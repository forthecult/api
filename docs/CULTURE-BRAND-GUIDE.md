## PREAMBLE: WHAT THIS BRAND ACTUALLY IS

Culture is not a store. It is a point of view about how to live.

It began in 2015 selling Bitcoin tees to cypherpunks. It shut down in 2019. It came back as something larger: a curated collection of products that protect your health and expand your autonomy — from organic cotton apparel to hardware wallets, from mycotoxin-tested coffee to GrapheneOS phones.

The crypto community taught this brand everything it knows: self-sovereignty, privacy, quality over quantity, skepticism of centralized systems. Now it brings those values to everyone — including people who have never heard of Solana.

**The brand sits at a specific intersection:**
- Health-conscious, but not granola
- Tech-literate, but not tech-bro
- Premium, but not aspirational luxury
- Principled, but not preachy
- Community-driven, but not populist
- Crypto-native, but crypto is a means — never the identity

**The feeling when you encounter Culture should be:**
The quiet confidence of someone who has already done the research. They are not trying to convince you. But if you are here, you already know.

## Color Palette

The palette is restrained by design. Let the products provide color. The brand provides the frame.

### Obsidian
- **Hex:** `#111111`
- **Mood:** Authority, precision, depth
- **Usage:** Primary background (dark contexts), headlines, primary text on light backgrounds, footer. The brand's default surface. Not pure black — there is warmth in it, just barely.

### Parchment
- **Hex:** `#F5F1EB`
- **Mood:** Warmth, breathing room, considered luxury
- **Usage:** Light-mode backgrounds, card surfaces, page canvas. Warmer than pure white. Feels like premium uncoated paper — the kind a well-made zine would be printed on. Never stark white (#FFF) for large surfaces.

### Smoke
- **Hex:** `#8A857E`
- **Mood:** Restraint, sophistication, supporting role
- **Usage:** Secondary text, captions, metadata, muted UI elements, borders on light backgrounds. The brand's quiet workhorse. Carries information without demanding attention.

### Amber
- **Hex:** `#C4873A`
- **Mood:** Craft, warmth, earth, intentionality
- **Usage:** Primary accent. CTAs, links, hover states, selected/active indicators, badges, price highlights. The only "loud" color in the system. Use it like a stamp — sparingly, with purpose. When Amber appears, it means: *pay attention to this*.

### Sage
- **Hex:** `#6B8F71`
- **Mood:** Health, growth, trust, natural order
- **Usage:** Health-related content, success states, "in stock" indicators, the Health pillar. Connects the brand to its wellness identity without being clinical green.

### Signal
- **Hex:** `#B5594E`
- **Mood:** Urgency, attention, human warmth
- **Usage:** Warnings, out-of-stock, error states, limited-edition callouts, sale pricing. Not aggressive red — muted, earthy, like terracotta. Signals importance without screaming.

### Usage rules:
- Light mode: Parchment canvas, Obsidian text, Amber accents.
- Dark mode: Obsidian canvas, Parchment text, Amber accents. Smoke lightens to `#A9A49D` for secondary text.
- Never use more than one accent color (Amber, Sage, or Signal) in the same visual block.
- Product imagery provides all other color. The brand frame stays neutral.

## Typography Personality

### Primary: Heading & UI — *Manrope*

**Family:** [Manrope](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Manrope) (Google Fonts, open source)

**Mood:** Geometric confidence with subtle warmth. Modern without being cold. It has the precision of a Swiss typeface but the approachability of a humanist one. It says: "We know exactly what we are doing, and we are comfortable with that."

**Weights & Usage:**
| Context | Weight | Size guidance |
|---------|--------|---------------|
| Hero headlines (H1) | ExtraBold (800) | 3rem–3.5rem (48–56px) |
| Section titles (H2) | Bold (700) | 2rem–2.5rem (32–40px) |
| Card titles (H3) | SemiBold (600) | 1.25rem–1.5rem (20–24px) |
| Navigation, buttons | Medium (500) | 0.875rem–1rem (14–16px) |

**Rules:**
- Headlines: sentence case, never ALL CAPS (except the brand name CULTURE in specific lockups). ALL CAPS headlines feel like shouting. This brand does not shout.
- Letter-spacing: Default or slightly tightened (`-0.01em` to `-0.02em`) for large headlines. Never expanded.
- Line-height: 1.1 for headlines, 1.3 for subheadings.

### Secondary: Body & Long-form — *Inter* or *Geist*

**Family:** [Inter](https://rsms.me/inter/) or Geist (currently in use)

**Mood:** Invisible by design. The reader should never notice the body font — they should only notice the words. Both Inter and Geist are Swiss-lineage sans-serifs with exceptional readability at small sizes and massive weight ranges.

**Weights & Usage:**
| Context | Weight | Size |
|---------|--------|------|
| Body copy | Regular (400) | 1rem (16px) |
| Emphasized body | Medium (500) | 1rem (16px) |
| Captions, metadata | Regular (400) | 0.875rem (14px) |
| Fine print, labels | Regular (400) | 0.75rem (12px) |

**Rules:**
- Line-height: 1.6 for body copy. Generous. Give the words room.
- Max width for prose: 680px. Never let body text run edge-to-edge.
- Paragraph spacing: 1.5em between paragraphs. No indent.

### Accent: Data & Crypto — *JetBrains Mono*

**Family:** [JetBrains Mono](https://www.jetbrains.com/lp/mono/) (already implemented)

**Mood:** Technical authority, precision, hacker lineage. When this font appears, it says: "This is data. This is exact. We come from code."

**Usage:**
- All crypto prices and conversions (BTC, SOL, XAG, CULT amounts)
- Order IDs, tracking numbers, transaction hashes
- Technical specifications (weight, dimensions, materials lists)
- Code blocks, API documentation
- Footer copyright / legal micro-text (optional, adds character)

**Rules:**
- Never use for headlines or marketing copy. This font is a specialist.
- Size: typically 0.875rem (14px) or smaller. It is an accent, not a voice.
- Color: often Smoke on light backgrounds, muted on dark. It should feel embedded, not highlighted.

## Visual Language

Culture's visual identity is **editorial restraint with quiet technical confidence**. Think: a well-curated independent magazine about how to live — laid out by someone who trained in Swiss design but lives in Joshua Tree.

**The aesthetic is defined by generous negative space.** Every element earns its place. If something can be removed without losing meaning, it should be removed. The white space is not emptiness — it is a statement that says: *we are not desperate for your attention*.

**Photography is documentary, not commercial.** Products are shown in real environments — on a kitchen counter, worn on a walk, next to a Berkey filter in an actual apartment. Natural lighting. Slight warmth in the color grade. Never glossy, never over-retouched. The people in photographs look like they actually use these products, not like they were cast from a modeling agency. Hands, textures, and close-up details matter more than wide lifestyle shots.

**Composition favors asymmetry and off-center framing.** Not everything is centered. Important elements often sit at the left third or the top third. This creates visual tension that holds attention without being disorienting. Grids are used, but they breathe — items do not fill every cell.

**Texture is subtle and tactile.** Think: the grain of organic cotton close-up, the patina on a well-used leather wallet, the slight unevenness of hand-poured dark chocolate. The brand acknowledges that premium products have physical presence. Flat digital illustration is used sparingly and only for diagrams or icons — never as decoration.

**The overall impression is: considered, warm, unhurried.** A site visit should feel like walking into a shop run by someone who curates carefully and does not rush you. There is nothing flashing, nothing spinning, nothing urgently demanding a click. The confidence of the curation is the persuasion.

### Mood board keywords:
Linen texture. Morning light through a window. A well-organized desk. Japanese packaging. Aesop store interiors. An independent bookshop. Matte finishes. Handwritten shipping labels. Warm concrete. A single perfect object on a wooden surface.

### In:
- Real photography with natural light
- Close-up product texture shots
- Muted, warm color grading
- Generous margins and whitespace
- Clean data presentation (tables, specs)
- Subtle grain or paper texture overlays (sparingly)
- Product-in-context photography

### Out:
- Stock photography of any kind
- Neon colors, bright gradients, "crypto-bro" aesthetics
- Overly polished/airbrushed lifestyle imagery
- Busy patterns, excessive iconography
- Floating 3D renders or metaverse-style visuals
- Parallax scrolling or aggressive animation
- Generic e-commerce "trust badge" strips

## Logo Direction

### Concept 1: "The Wordmark" — CULTURE

A clean, spaced wordmark in Manrope ExtraBold (or a custom-drawn geometric sans). All capitals. Generous letter-spacing (+0.08em to +0.12em). No symbol, no icon.

**What it communicates:** Confidence through simplicity. The brand name alone is the identity. It says: the word means something to us, and we are not hiding behind a symbol. References: AESOP, MUJI, COS.

**When to use:** Primary logo on homepage header, about page, packaging, social media profiles, email headers. This is the default.

**Refinement:** Optionally, the 'U' in CULTURE can have a very subtle flattened bottom, making it almost like a vessel or container — a nod to curation without being literal.

### Concept 2: "The Monogram" — Ct

A ligature of C and t, drawn from Manrope's geometry. Lowercase 't' tucked into the uppercase 'C'. Simple, compact, recognizable at small sizes.

**What it communicates:** Intimacy and craft. The ligature suggests connection — two letters leaning on each other, like community. It is personal, not corporate.

**When to use:** Favicon, app icon, small-space applications (social media avatar, embroidered on apparel, watermark on photography, shipping labels).

### Concept 3: "The Vessel" — Abstract mark

A minimal geometric shape: an open-topped rectangle or arch — like a container, a doorway, or a vessel. Clean lines, no fill. Paired with the CULTURE wordmark below it.

**What it communicates:** Curation (a vessel that holds carefully chosen things), threshold (an invitation to enter), autonomy (an open structure, not a closed box). It is abstract enough to scale across categories — health, tech, travel — without being specific to any one.

**When to use:** Brand mark for partnerships, co-branded collaborations, physical product tags, brand stamps. When you need a symbol that works without the name.

### Logo rules:
- Minimum clear space: the height of the 'C' on all sides
- Never rotate, stretch, add effects, or place on busy backgrounds
- On dark backgrounds: Parchment or white
- On light backgrounds: Obsidian
- Amber accent is never used in the logo itself

## Brand Don'ts — 10 Rules the Brand Never Breaks

1. **Never use pure white (#FFFFFF) as a background.** Use Parchment or a contextual off-white. Pure white is sterile. This brand is warm.

2. **Never use ALL CAPS for body copy or long headlines.** Sentence case only. The brand does not yell. (Exception: the CULTURE wordmark logo lockup and the CULT token name.)

3. **Never use stock photography.** Every image is either original photography, product shots, or user-submitted with permission. If it looks like it came from Shutterstock, it is wrong.

4. **Never describe a product as "revolutionary," "game-changing," or "disrupting."** The brand states facts. The customer decides if it is revolutionary.

5. **Never use more than one accent color in the same visual block.** The palette is restrained for a reason. If Amber is the CTA, Sage does not also appear in the same card.

6. **Never animate anything that does not need to move.** No auto-playing carousels, no bouncing badges, no pulsing buttons. Motion is used for transitions (page load, hover feedback, scroll reveals) — never for decoration.

7. **Never place text over a photograph without a sufficient contrast layer.** If text overlays an image, use a gradient overlay or solid panel. Readability is non-negotiable.

8. **Never use crypto jargon without context.** If a term like "staking" or "token-gated" appears, it is either explained inline or linked to an explanation. The brand is accessible to someone who has never held crypto.

9. **Never fake scarcity or urgency.** No countdown timers unless a sale is real. No "Only 2 left!" unless inventory is genuinely that low. Trust is the brand's most important asset.

10. **Never let the design compete with the product.** The website, the packaging, the brand system — all of it is a frame. The product is the painting. If someone remembers the animation more than the product, the design failed.

## Voice Personality — Four Dimensions

### 1. Considered (not impulsive)

**Description:** Every sentence has been thought through. No filler words, no padding, no "just" or "really" or "very." The brand speaks like someone who edits before they talk.

- **Do:** "Natural fibers breathe better and don't shed microplastics."
- **Don't:** "We really truly believe in the amazing power of natural fibers to help you live your best life!"

### 2. Direct (not blunt)

**Description:** The brand says what it means in the fewest possible words. But it is never rude or dismissive. Directness is a form of respect — it assumes the reader is intelligent and does not need hand-holding.

- **Do:** "Your data is yours. We delete it when you ask."
- **Don't:** "We're passionate about championing your digital sovereignty in today's complex data-driven ecosystem."

### 3. Knowing (not condescending)

**Description:** The brand clearly has a point of view and has done its homework. It shares knowledge as a peer, not as a teacher. It references the *why* behind its choices without lecturing. There is an implicit "we've looked into this, and here's what we found" quality.

- **Do:** "Polyester is linked to endocrine disruption. We only sell natural fibers."
- **Don't:** "Did you know that the clothes you wear could be slowly poisoning you? Let us educate you about the dangers of synthetic fabrics."

### 4. Warm (not soft)

**Description:** The brand has genuine warmth — it cares about its customers and its community. But it never drifts into saccharine, performative kindness. The warmth comes from specificity and honesty, not from exclamation marks and emojis.

- **Do:** "We test every batch of coffee for mycotoxins. Most brands don't."
- **Don't:** "We're SO excited to bring you the most AMAZING coffee experience ever!! You deserve it!! 🎉☕"

## Writing Rules

**Sentence length:** Short by default. Average 12–18 words. Vary rhythm — a short declarative sentence followed by a slightly longer explanatory one. Never exceed 30 words without a very good reason.

**Punctuation:** Periods over exclamation marks. Em dashes (—) for asides. Colons for setup: payoff structure. Semicolons are fine; this brand is literate. Ellipses only in intentional trailing-off, never for suspense.

**Capitalization:** Sentence case everywhere. The brand name "Culture" is capitalized. The token name "CULT" is all caps (it is a ticker symbol). Product category names are lowercase ("health & wellness," not "Health & Wellness") except at the start of a sentence.

**Humor:** Dry, understated, occasional. Never forced. The brand can be wry — a raised eyebrow, not a wink. If a joke needs an emoji to land, it is not a good joke.

**Urgency:** The brand almost never creates urgency. When it does (genuine limited edition, real deadline), it states the fact plainly: "50 units. When they're gone, they're gone." No countdown timers. No "HURRY!" No fake scarcity.

**Self-reference:** "We" is natural and human. "Culture" for formal contexts. Never "the Culture team" (sounds corporate) or "us here at Culture" (sounds like a small-town car dealership). The brand refers to CULT holders as "members" or "holders," never "our community of amazing supporters."

**Numbers:** Use numerals for prices, quantities, and specs. Spell out numbers one through nine in prose. Use the monospace font (JetBrains Mono) for crypto amounts, order IDs, and technical data.

**Product descriptions:** Lead with what the product does, not what it is. "Filters 99.9% of contaminants from tap water" before "Stainless steel gravity-fed filtration system." Benefits first, specifications second.

## UX & Design Direction

**Layout density:** Low. The site should feel spacious, not packed. A homepage section should contain one idea, one headline, one visual — not three cards crammed into a carousel. The default instinct should be "can we remove something?" not "can we add something?"

**White space:** Generous and deliberate. Margins of 80–120px between major sections on desktop. Padding within cards: 24–32px. The whitespace is not wasted space — it is the brand's way of saying: we are not desperate for your attention. Every element has room to breathe.

**Animation philosophy:** Purposeful and restrained. Acceptable: fade-in on scroll (200–300ms, opacity only), smooth hover transitions on cards and buttons (150–200ms), page transition fades. Not acceptable: parallax scrolling, auto-playing carousels, bouncing elements, particle effects, scroll-jacking. If something moves, it should be because the user caused it (hover, click, scroll) — not because the page decided to perform.

**Mobile experience:** Mobile is not a scaled-down desktop — it is the primary reading experience. Touch targets: 44px minimum. Cards stack cleanly to single-column. Navigation collapses to a drawer with the same labels as desktop (no separate mobile taxonomy). Product images are swipeable. The "Add to Cart" button is always reachable without scrolling. Page weight on mobile should target under 1.5MB for initial load.

**Imagery-to-copy ratio:** Roughly 60/40 on product pages (image-heavy), 40/60 on content pages (copy-heavy), 50/50 on the homepage. Photography is large and prominent but never decorative — every image earns its space by showing a product, a person using a product, or a material detail.

**Overall emotional tone when navigating:** Calm authority. The site should feel like walking into a well-lit shop with a knowledgeable owner who does not follow you around. There is no urgency, no noise, no pop-ups. The products are presented clearly. The information is there when you need it. The checkout is fast and private. You leave feeling like you made a good decision — not like you were sold to.

## Constraints Summary

**Tone:** Quiet confidence. Considered warmth. Dry wit when appropriate.

**Avoid:** Hype language. Buzzwords. Generic empowerment copy. Crypto-bro energy. Neon gradients. Aggressive animation. Stock photography. Fake urgency. Corporate-speak.

**The brand should feel like:** It has a specific, earned point of view — not like it is trying to appeal to everyone. It is for the intentional consumer. Everyone else will catch up eventually.
